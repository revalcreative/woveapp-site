The Complete Guide to Organic Cotton

What it is, how to spot it, and how to shop for it without falling for the marketing.

Cotton sounds simple. It's a plant. You grow it, spin it into yarn, weave it into fabric, and wear it. That's been the basic story for thousands of years.

What's changed in the last seventy or so years is what happens between the plant and the shirt. Conventional cotton is now one of the most chemically intensive crops in modern agriculture — heavily treated with synthetic pesticides, insecticides, fertilizers, and defoliants — and the fabric it becomes typically goes through dozens of additional chemical processing steps before it reaches a hanger. Organic cotton is the alternative version of that supply chain. Same plant. Very different process.

This guide walks through what organic cotton actually is, how it differs from conventional cotton, what the certifications mean (and which ones to actually trust), where it shines, where it has limits, and how to shop for it without getting greenwashed in the process.

What is organic cotton?

Organic cotton is cotton grown according to certified organic farming standards — meaning without synthetic pesticides, synthetic fertilizers, or genetically modified seeds. The fiber must come from non-GMO plants, and the fields must be free of prohibited synthetic chemicals for at least three years before the cotton can be certified.

But there's a layer that surprises people: the term "organic" technically refers only to the growing of the cotton, not what happens after. A cotton plant grown organically can still be processed using chlorine bleach, formaldehyde, azo dyes, and other harmful chemicals in the spinning, weaving, dyeing, and finishing stages. This is why certifications like GOTS exist — to extend organic standards across the entire supply chain rather than just the field.

The basic principles behind organic cotton farming:

  • No synthetic pesticides or insecticides. Pest control relies on biological methods (beneficial insects, neem oil, trap crops) and cultural practices (crop rotation, mixed planting).
  • No synthetic fertilizers. Fertility is built through composting, cover cropping, and animal manure.
  • No GMO seeds. Organic cotton must be grown from non-genetically modified seed.
  • Three-year transition. Land must be free of prohibited substances for three years before a harvest can be certified organic.
  • Soil health as a priority. Organic farming actively builds soil structure and biodiversity rather than depleting it.

Organic cotton vs. conventional cotton

The most useful way to understand organic cotton is to put it next to its conventional counterpart and look at what's actually different across the lifecycle.

A few things worth knowing about the comparison:

On the pesticide stat. Organic Authority and other widely-cited sources put cotton at 24% of the world's insecticide market and 11% of global pesticide sales, making it one of the most pesticide-intensive crops on the planet. More recent figures from the Environmental Justice Foundation revise that down to 16% of insecticides and 6% of pesticides. Either way, the headline holds: cotton uses a disproportionate share of global agricultural chemicals relative to the land it occupies.

On water. The water comparison is contested. Older sources cite the 20,000-liters-per-kilogram figure. Recent data revises that downward to roughly 2,068 liters per kilogram on average, with wide regional variation. Organic cotton typically uses meaningfully less water than conventional — TheRoundup's compiled industry data suggests organic cotton can use up to 91% less water — but the exact gap depends a lot on how and where the cotton is grown.

On processing. This is the most overlooked piece. A garment labeled "organic cotton" on its own says only that the fiber was grown organically. It doesn't say anything about how the fabric was bleached, dyed, or finished. For that, you need GOTS — which is what the next section is about.

Organic cotton certifications: what they mean

Cotton labeling is one of the most marketed and least regulated parts of clothing. Anyone can put "organic cotton" on a hangtag. What separates a meaningful claim from a marketing claim is a third-party certification with an audit trail.

How to think about which certification matters when:

  • GOTS "Organic" is what you want for items where chemistry matters most — anything against your skin for long periods, anything for babies and kids, anything you'll wash and wear repeatedly. The 95% organic threshold combined with full-supply-chain chemistry standards is the most protective label available.
  • GOTS "Made with Organic" is genuinely meaningful and not a downgrade in processing standards — just a lower fiber threshold to accommodate small percentages of non-organic materials (often elastane in basics like underwear, or recycled fibers in blends). For categories that require stretch, this is often the highest level you'll realistically find.
  • OCS verifies fiber content but doesn't restrict chemical processing. A garment certified OCS-only could still be processed with formaldehyde or chlorine bleach. Useful, but not a complete picture.
  • OEKO-TEX Standard 100 isn't an organic certification at all — it's a test on the finished garment for harmful substance limits. Pairing GOTS or OCS with OEKO-TEX gives you both upstream sourcing assurance and downstream chemistry assurance.

What to be skeptical of: an "organic cotton" claim with no certification logo, license number, or third-party seal. The U.S. doesn't tightly regulate the word "organic" on clothing tags the way it does on food, so unverified claims are common.

Benefits of organic cotton

The case for organic cotton works on three levels — environmental, personal, and social — and the relative weight depends on what you care most about.

Environmental benefits. No synthetic pesticide and fertilizer runoff into local water systems. Healthier soil that retains carbon and supports biodiversity. Less reliance on the petrochemical industry that produces most synthetic agricultural inputs. Lower water use in many cases. Better farm-level adaptability to changing climate conditions because of more resilient soil.

Personal exposure. A finished organic cotton garment — especially a GOTS-certified one — carries far lower residues of the chemicals that conventional cotton typically passes through: pesticide residue from the field, plus formaldehyde, chlorine bleach, heavy metals, and certain azo dyes from conventional processing. This matters most for items in extended skin contact: underwear, bras, base layers, sheets, towels, and anything for babies, whose skin is more permeable and whose bodies are still developing.

Social and farmer welfare. Conventional cotton production has well-documented links to acute pesticide poisoning among farmworkers, particularly in lower-income countries where protective equipment is less available and pesticide regulations are weaker. Organic farming dramatically reduces those exposures. GOTS specifically adds International Labour Organization-based social criteria — fair wages, freedom of association, no child or forced labor — that apply across the entire supply chain, not just the field.

For most of us, the biggest tangible difference at home is going to be the chemistry of the finished garment. Cotton that has been grown organically and processed under GOTS standards genuinely doesn't have the same chemical residue profile as a conventionally grown and conventionally processed cotton garment — particularly for items that touch your skin all day.

Where to start with organic cotton

The goal isn't to overhaul your closet overnight. The most useful approach is to prioritize the categories where organic cotton genuinely changes the chemistry of what's against your body, and let the rest catch up over time as items wear out.

The framework is simple: the longer something is against your skin, and the more permeable that skin is, the more the upgrade pays off. That order in practice:

  1. Underwear, bras, base layers, and baby/toddler clothes. This is where to start. These items sit against the most sensitive skin for the most hours per day, and get washed and re-worn most frequently — so any chemical residue from conventional processing has the most opportunity to migrate. For more on this category specifically, see our guide to non-toxic basics.
  2. Bedding, pajamas, and loungewear. You spend roughly a third of your life against your sheets and in your pajamas. Switching these to GOTS-certified organic cotton is a high-leverage move.
  3. Casual wear — t-shirts, soft pants, knit dresses. Daily moderate skin contact. Lower urgency than the first two tiers, but a meaningful win as items wear out and get replaced.
  4. Outerwear and rarely-laundered items. Lowest priority. A wool blazer or a denim jacket isn't a typical PFAS or microplastic concern — and isn't where organic cotton has the strongest case either.

When in doubt, check what's actually in a garment with the Wove app before you buy. The label doesn't always tell you, and the marketing definitely doesn't.

Where organic cotton has limits

Organic cotton solves real problems, but it isn't a silver bullet. A few honest caveats worth knowing:

It doesn't address microplastic shedding if the garment is blended with synthetic fibers. A "70% organic cotton, 30% polyester" t-shirt still sheds polyester microplastics. The organic label only governs the cotton portion. For more on this dynamic, see our microplastics in clothing guide.

"Organic cotton" alone doesn't guarantee chemical-free finishing. Without a GOTS or OEKO-TEX backing, the fiber may have been grown organically and then bleached, dyed, and finished using the same chemicals as conventional cotton. The certification is the part that matters.

Yields are lower. Organic cotton typically produces less fiber per acre than conventional cotton, which is part of why prices are higher and why farmer transition takes investment. This is a real economic challenge, not just a marketing line.

Not all organic cotton is created equal. Organic farming in a water-stressed region with poor labor protections is meaningfully different from organic farming on a regenerative farm in a temperate climate. Certifications help, but country and brand transparency still matter.

Price. Genuinely organic and GOTS-certified clothing typically costs 2–3× what conventional cotton equivalents do. That's a real barrier. The two ways to make it sustainable for most households: prioritize the highest-impact categories first (basics), and let everything else catch up over time as items wear out.

Frequently asked questions

What is organic cotton?

Organic cotton is cotton grown according to certified organic farming standards — without synthetic pesticides, synthetic fertilizers, or genetically modified seeds. To be certified organic, the land must be free of prohibited synthetic chemicals for at least three years before harvest. When combined with a certification like GOTS, organic standards extend across the entire supply chain — including spinning, weaving, dyeing, and finishing.

What's the difference between organic cotton and regular cotton?

Conventional cotton is grown with synthetic pesticides, insecticides, and fertilizers, typically from genetically modified seeds, and is often processed with chemicals like chlorine bleach, formaldehyde, and azo dyes. Organic cotton is grown without any of those inputs and — if GOTS-certified — is also processed without the harmful chemicals commonly used in conventional finishing.

What does GOTS organic cotton mean?

GOTS stands for Global Organic Textile Standard. A garment labeled "Organic" under GOTS must contain at least 95% certified organic fibers; a garment labeled "Made with Organic" must contain at least 70%. Both grades require the same standards on chemical processing, wastewater treatment, and worker welfare across the entire supply chain. GOTS is the most comprehensive organic textile certification currently available.

Is organic cotton actually better for the environment?

Organic cotton farming eliminates synthetic pesticide and fertilizer runoff, supports soil health and biodiversity, and typically uses less water than conventional cotton. The size of the water savings depends a lot on where and how the cotton is grown. The environmental case is strongest when paired with full-supply-chain certifications like GOTS that also restrict downstream chemistry.

Is organic cotton worth the price?

For categories with the most skin contact — underwear, bras, base layers, baby and toddler clothes, bedding, pajamas — the chemistry difference between conventionally processed cotton and GOTS-certified organic cotton is meaningful, and worth the premium for many households. For categories with less skin contact or less frequent washing, the case is weaker. The most practical approach is to prioritize basics first.

How do I know if cotton is really organic?

Look for a certification logo plus a license number. The two most credible: GOTS (full supply chain, includes chemistry and labor standards) and the Organic Content Standard (OCS) (verifies fiber content but not processing). Pair either with OEKO-TEX Standard 100 for finished-product chemistry testing. An "organic cotton" claim without a certification is essentially unverified.

Does organic cotton shed microplastics?

No — cotton is a natural cellulose fiber and doesn't shed plastic microfibers. However, many garments labeled as containing organic cotton are blends with polyester, nylon, or elastane. The synthetic portion does shed microplastics. If microplastic shedding is your priority, look for 100% organic cotton (or "Made with Organic" with the remaining percentage being natural fibers like elastane, which is a very small fraction).

What's the best organic cotton certification?

GOTS at the "Organic" label grade (95%+ organic fibers) is the most comprehensive certification available and the strongest single signal of quality. For garments that require stretch, GOTS at the "Made with Organic" grade (70%+) is usually the highest realistic level. Pairing either with OEKO-TEX Standard 100 adds finished-product chemistry assurance.
